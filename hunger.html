Hunger

Hunger is a world crisis. It is not something that can truly be solved by just one nation or even a handful of nations. First world countries even still face a disproportionate amount of hunger in their countries. Which is why a country like Brazil which is still developing, is facing the brunt of hunger. They clear natural resources to make way for grazing animals such as cattle and then export a large amount of that resource out to other countries. Unemployment has been on the rise due to the covid-19 pandemic. The price of everyday goods and foods are continuing to rise despite stagnant wages and lack of employment opportunities.

Hunger is perhaps one of the biggest issues facing Brazil. Many other facets they have been able to stem through actions taken. Hunger, however, has continued to grow with over half the country facing food insecurities in 2020. According to Milhorance of the New York Times, “… about 117 million people, or roughly 55 percent of the country’s population, faced food insecurity, with uncertain access to enough nutrition”1. This is up from previous years by a significant margin. There was a bit of relief in the last year with Covid-19 relief measures.

However, these measures have been substandard and irregular to keep up with the growing concerns caused by the pandemic compounded with economic and social conditions that are straining the Brazilian people. New internationalist wrote in an article, “Despite the federal cash transfer program, emergency welfare has been inconsistent…; insufficient…, and unable to assist those in need..”2. With this shortage in income, the populace is having trouble affording things like fresh produce and fruits. This diminishing the overall nutrition that the people of Brazil are consuming on average. Milhorance points out, “The cost of cooking oil more than tripled. A kilogram of rice goes for twice as much. Brazil exports one-fifth of its beef to other countries rather than using it to feed its own populace.

This serves to expand the wealth inequality gap between the wealthy and those below the poverty line. The government having shirked its responsibility to guarantee the securities of its citizen is believed to be a primary reason for the growing number of those that face food insecurity. There is seems to be a vested interested in profit over people as the government doesn’t subsidize food for its people or provide a stable and adequate means of procuring the necessities monetarily. As Philips points out in his The Guardian article, “Latin America’s most populous nation, where researchers say 19 million people have gone hungry since the start of {the} Covid outbreak..”3.
