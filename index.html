Poverty in Brazil

Poverty

In Brazil in the year of 2019, nearly twenty percent of the population was underneath the poverty line, which means that the average person was living on an average of under six dollars a day. Prices are continuing to rise as time progresses, with essentials surging the cost of living. In addition to the cost of living rising, Brazil’s unemployment rate has been reaching new heights. This contributes to the widening inequalities of wealth between those below the poverty line and those in the top one percent. This overarching issue is compounded by issues in infrastructure, access to sanitation and clean water, and hunger.

Infastructure

When covid hit Brazil in 2020, the homeless population numbered 149,019. By April 2021, the figure had shot up to 162,045, an 8.7 percent increase. Roadways are vastly outdated, many homes are in unlivable conditions, and education is far behind as there is a lack of funding for schools. To help combat this, Brazil has been heavily betting on funding from increasing business partners investing in Brazil’s infrastructure. However, COVID-19 affected everyone drastically and has caused many investments to be put on hold as companies are having difficulties with cash.

Water and Sanitation

The lack of clean water and sanitation has been an ongoing issue in Brazil. Over 100 million residents have been living without access to proper sanitation. Roughly 15 million Brazilians in urban areas and 25 million in rural areas live without safe water. In the most poverty-stricken areas, the favelas, lack the structures for any water or sanitation. Lengthy droughts from climate change have created a significant water crisis in Brazil.

Hunger

A large portion of the Brazilian population face food insecurities. There are several reasons for this. They export a large portion of their domestically grown resources such as cattle. Unemployment is on the rise reducing the amount of income for the average person. The Covid-19 pandemic increased the rate of unemployment and there is a surge in prices of everyday essentials thus straining the system even more raising the risk of food insecurity.
