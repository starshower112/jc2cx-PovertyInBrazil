Infrastructure


Infrastructure in Brazil has proven to be an issue for years. Their outdated roads, expensive transportation methods, and lack of funding have caused the country to be severely behind other countries with their infrastructure. However, in recent years Brazil’s government has begun to prioritize its outdated infrastructure and has made some significant improvements, including increasing their global infrastructure rating. With new interest in business investors, Brazil has major plans to completely revitalize the infrastructure of the country.


As of the World Bank's 2018-2019 Logistics Performance Index, Brazil ranks 56th out of 160 countries in terms of the quality of its infrastructure1. Brazil has a continuing issue of expensive operational costs resulting from numerous factors, many having to do with the inefficiency of its infrastructure and its highly disorganized economy. One of the factors of these high operational costs is cargo travel. Brazil carries out a majority of its cargo travel by trucks, a far more expensive method of transportation that contributes heavily to their already large logistics cost. These logistics costs for Brazil make up around 12% of the total GDP (Gross Domestic Product) for Brazil1. Considering 60% of Brazil's freight moves by road3, it doesn't seem Brazil has ever concerned themselves much with the upkeep of thier highways. From 2011-2014 Brazil allocated less than 1% of its total GDP on highway maintenance3. To give context to this number, the World Bank claimed that they would need to allocated about 6% of GDP in order to catch up with advanced industrial countries3. In 2020, World Bank did a study and discovered that of 1.75 million km of highways, only 18% of those highways were paved4. With not only 60% of freight travel, but also the world's fourth largest highway and road system, you have to expect Brazil to take current and future funding to heavily improve its highway infrastructure.

On top of expensive transportation costs and lack of funds for supporting the infrastructure of the country, investments in Brazilian infrastructure reached a decade low in 2020, with an amount of 123 Billion Brazilian reals2. Brazil relies heavily on external investors to fund the divisions of their country, and with those investments decreasing, many future infrastructure plans including 45 new airports, 14,540 km of total concessions improving railways, 21 terminals for cargo boats, and 18,121.05 km of roadway improvements all totaling around $44.66 billion USD1 were put on a hiatus until funding returned to normal.


But not all is bad in terms of Brazilian infrastructure, Brazil is actually showing tremendous signs of recovery since Covid began. The Government of Brazil has put together a projects pipeline that stretches over the next few years. These projects have already begun being presented to Congress and external investors with hopes of receiving funding to further the countries development and attract investments for future development. One of these projects is the privatization of the Port of Santos supported by the Brazilian Development Bank1. This move into private port management is expected to generate a greater flow of investments, bring more dynamism to port activity, as well as modernize and improve service1. The future of Brazil is looking extremely promising, and if as much attention is put into the other divisions listed on this website as it is into the future of infrastructure, then in just a few short years Brazil will have a completely new look.
